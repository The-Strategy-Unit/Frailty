Analysis of descriptive statistics supporting impact evaluation of the Frailty Improvement Collaborative

Overview

Introduction

This document provides a descriptive overview of the key metrics related to the impact evaluation of the Frailty Improvement Collaborative.

Identifying places

All outcomes were constructed by linking the outcomes of individual patients back to their GP practices, with GP practices then allocated to geographical places, in order to construct outcomes for each place.

Interventions happened at the level of ‘place’. This aligned with sub-ICB locations as used internally by ICBs. However, the available national datasets use a definition and boundary of sub-ICB locations which do not necessarily match the internal definition used by that ICB. For example, in the available national datasets the Black Country ICB has a single sub-ICB location which covers the whole ICB - and is not broken down any further. For this reason it was not possible to directly use the most recent sub-ICB locations as defined in national datasets to identify the discovery places (e.g. Wolverhampton in the case of the Black Country).

The CCG boundaries as defined in the financial year 2017/18 were instead used to identify places. In many cases the 2017/18 CCG areas map directly on to current sub-ICB locations. However in some cases, they provided breakdowns better aligned with the definition of places as used by this programme. For example, the 2017/18 CCG boundaries break down the Black Country ICB into multiple sub-locations, one of which was Wolverhampton (which was one of the places which received the intervention).

In the case of Gloucestershire, the defined ‘place’ was a PCN (North and South Gloucester). For the purpose of this analysis, in order to make results comparable to other areas, the affected area was taken to be the Integrated Locality Partnership (ILP) that the PCN sat within (Gloucester). Even though it includes a slightly larger area (taking in other parts of Gloucester) it remains one of the smaller places.

Using the 2017/18 CCG boundaries, there would be 207 places, although due to Gloucestershire being broken down into six ILPs (rather than the one sub-ICB in Gloucestershire ICB), the country is instead broken down into 212 places. Seven ICBs introduced the programme, although in some ICBs, two places were listed as taking part in the programme, resulting in a total of ten places which took part in the programme.

A small number of GPs were excluded due to the process of allocating GPs to places - for example, they were created since 2018 and were allocated to sub-ICB codes which do not have a direct counterpart in the 2017/18 CCG boundaries. Some GPs were also excluded due to not aligning directly with a geographic region (i.e. the GP code is exclusively used for military service personnel or for homeless individuals across an ICB).

Definition of frailty

For the purposes of this analysis, patients were defined as frail if they had an intermediate risk or high risk based on their Hospital Frailty Score (HFS), where the HFS was constructed for each patient at any given point in time constructed using their diagnoses within the preceding two years.

Variables used

The available data extended to April 2018, although outcomes for frail patients were misleadingly low in the early months because there was not enough preceding data to accurately identify frail patients on former diagnoses. For this reason, all outcomes are shown from April 2019, meaning there is at least a full year’s worth of data with which to identify which patients are frail.

The following descriptive variables are included here:

  • Frail population size,

  • Frailty-weighted deprivation score,

  • Frailty-weighted rural index score.

The following outcomes are included here as potential outcomes for an impact evaluation:

  • Reduction in hospital admissions for frail patients,

  • Reduced average length of stay for frail patients,

  • Fewer total bed days used by frail patients,

  • Reduction in delays to discharge and faster transitions to intermediate care,

  • Reduced attendances to Emergency Departments for frail patients,

  • Fewer hospital re-admissions within 28 days of discharge,

  • Reduction in hospital conveyances for frail patients,

  • Proportionally more discharges to usual place of residence.

Descriptive variables

Frail population

The frail population was determined by counting all of the individuals within a given place who had a HFS of five or higher at any point during the period covered.

Across all included places, across the entire period covered (from April 2019 to October 2025), there were 7,720,000 individuals who were identified as being frail at any point. Note that within the dataset used for analysis here, it appears to be 9,650,000 individuals, due to double-counting where frail individuals have changed GP. This issue should not cause any issues in later outcomes or the eventual analysis, as all outcomes focus on the number of attendances by frail individuals, rather than a count of the frail individuals themselves, and so should not be affected by individuals moving GP.

The average place has a mean of 46,000 frail individuals across the whole period, although this varies from a maximum of 167,000 to a minimum of 12,900 .

Frailty-weighted deprivation score

Places were given a frailty-weighted deprivation score by taking the proportion of frail patients within a place who live in the 20% most deprived Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs) in the country (using the 2025 Index of Multiple Deprivation).

Across all places, 22.8% of frail individuals lived in deprived LSOAs, reflecting the fact that frail individuals are slightly more likely to be deprived than the national average (20%). However this varies substantially by place, from 0.2% at the lowest to 87.6% at the highest.

Fewer hospital re-admissions for frail patients within 28 days of discharge

Hospital readmissions for frail patients within 28 days of discharge is a count of the total number of unplanned admissions to hospital by patients with a HFS of five or over at the time of admissions who also had a discharge within the preceding 28 days. It is worth noting that this definition also excludes patients with various conditions where treatment can involve repeated admission and discharge.

Reduction in delays to discharge and faster transitions to intermediate care for frail patients

Delays in discharge for frail patients was defined as the number of patients with a HFS of five or higher on admission, who were discharged after their Discharge Ready Date (DRD), as a proportion of all the discharged patients with a HFS of five or higher on admissions.

Hospital discharges are grouped into five categories, in relation to the patient’s discharge ready date (DRD):

  • discharged on the same day as admission

  • discharged after the DRD

  • discharged on the DRD

  • discharged before the DRD

  • no DRD.

The overall breakdown for discharge date for frail patients across all places across the full period is shown below.

Discharge Date Percentage
1 Discharged on admission date 56.9
2 No DRD 31.9
5 Discharged after DRD 3.8
4 Discharged on DRD 7.3
3 Discharged before DRD 0.1

The breakdown changes substantially over time, with the biggest change being a decrease in the number of discharges without a DRD (although nationally the number is still relatively high) and increases in the number of discharges on or after the DRD.

Although the proportion of discharges without a DRD has come down substantially, the fact that it is still so high and changes so much during the period the programme was implemented means that it is unlikely the proportion of discharges after the DRD would be a reliable measure if used as an outcome in any impact evaluation, as it is difficult to distinguish between changes in measurement (e.g. more patients having DRDs recorded) or a change in underlying true discharge outcomes (e.g. patients being discharged more promptly after being ready to be discharged), especially as different places show different trends to each other in recording a DRD.

Proportionally more discharges to usual place of residence for frail patients

Discharges to usual place of residence was defined as the number of patients with a HFS of five or higher on admission who were discharged to their usual place of residence, as a proportion of all discharged patients who had a HSF of five or higher on admission. Note that a number of patients were excluded from this analysis entirely due to having discharge location which was primarily caused by external circumstances - for example if their discharge location was a forced repatriation or was unknown.

Across all places, across the whole period, 96.4 % of frail patients were discharged to their usual place of residence.
